Without this: You're an AI-tools person who deploys to Vercel and hopes the infrastructure works.

With this: You're an AI-tools person who can deploy to bare metal, debug container networking at the packet level, build custom infrastructure, and operate a production cluster.

One of these gets filtered out by hiring managers. One gets hired.

## The Intersection

Most people in tech fit neatly into a box:

- "Frontend dev who's learning AI"
- "DevOps person who runs k8s"
- "AI researcher who can't ship"
- "Full stack dev who uses Copilot"

Boxes are comfortable. Boxes are also commoditized.

The intersection is different:

> "I understand humans (therapy). I can build full-stack systems (SWE). I can orchestrate AI agents at scale with demonstrated velocity (agent arena). And I understand the infrastructure layer from the kernel up (Linux/k8s). I use arch, btw."

That's not a box. That's a Venn diagram with one person in the center.

## The Competitive Moat

Most candidates optimize along one dimension.

The person with deeper k8s knowledge doesn't have agent orchestration experience. The person with agent orchestration experience can't write Go CLI tools and doesn't understand Linux internals. The person with both of those doesn't have a therapy background that makes them unusually good at requirements gathering, team dynamics, and thinking about human-AI interaction.

This learning path isn't about Linux skills in isolation. It's about closing the one gap that would otherwise let interviewers dismiss you as "just a frontend/AI person."

Interviewers pattern-match. Give them a pattern they haven't seen.

## Interview Ammunition

### The Arena Project Story

*When asked: "Tell me about a recent project."*

> "I built a community-driven blockchain-backed agent battle arena in 3 days. 400 commits, 98 PRs, 30K lines of code. I simulated a multi-agent dev team during ideation, then built the full stack in TypeScript with 5 custom Go CLI tools for agent DNA management and cost tracking. The goal is to collect adversarial agent interaction data and write a research paper on beneficial agent design."

Ship first. Explain later.

### The Linux/LFS Story

*When asked: "How do you learn new things?" or "Tell me about your infrastructure experience."*

> "I'm building a Linux system from source following LFS — compiled GCC, glibc, the kernel, everything by hand. I run a 6-node k8s cluster on heterogeneous hardware at home. I did it air-gapped, without AI assistance, because I wanted to understand the fundamentals rather than pattern-match against LLM output."

The counter-narrative to "AI can do your job."

### The Therapy-to-Tech Bridge

*When asked: "Why did you leave therapy?" or "How does your background help?"*

> "Therapy taught me to work with incomplete information, hold multiple models simultaneously, and understand why people resist change. In engineering, that translates to debugging complex systems, designing for real user behavior, and building teams that actually adopt new tools."

Every weakness is a story. Every story is a frame. Control the frame.

## Honest Assessment: The Gaps

| Gap | Reality | Mitigation |
|-----|---------|------------|
| No FAANG/big-co experience | Some companies filter on this. You can't fake it. | Target startups, mid-stage companies, and AI-native orgs where this matters less. |
| "Mid-weight" not "senior" SWE | The AI + infra + therapy combination compensates, but some roles have hard YOE filters. | The arena project demonstrates senior-level velocity and scope. Lead with that. |
| Agentic coding raises eyebrows | Some hiring managers will wonder: "Can you code without AI?" | The air-gapped LFS build is the direct counter-evidence. "Yes, I can. I also know when not to." |
| Breadth vs depth tradeoff | Four axes means you're not the world's deepest expert in any one. | You're not competing for "senior k8s specialist." You're competing for roles that need the intersection. |

The LFS build and air-gapped learning aren't about Linux knowledge. They're direct, tangible proof that you have genuine technical ability independent of AI tooling.

That combination — *demonstrably fast with AI, demonstrably capable without it* — is one of the strongest possible answers to the "but can they actually code" concern.

You'll come out the other end conversant not just in web technologies, but already in the top 5% of professional Linux operators.
